# Headline Channel Guide

Use this reference for any single line whose only job is to earn the next one: an essay or article title, a web page H1 or hero line, the first line of a LinkedIn post, an email subject line. A headline is not a section of a piece. It is the one line that decides whether the rest gets read.

This guide is cross-channel. A headline always sits on top of something written in another channel, so it borrows that channel's length and placement rules and adds its own craft on top. Read it alongside the channel the headline tops, not instead of it.

## Where the voice lives

This file describes how a headline works, not how each archetype sounds in one. For archetype voice (tone, rhythm, sentence patterns, vocabulary, anti-patterns), read the archetype file at `references/archetypes/<name>.md`. A Sage headline observes, a Rebel headline confronts, a Lover headline draws close. The headline carries the same voice as the body, concentrated into one line.

## The core rule: write it last

Write the headline after the piece is finished, never before.

A headline written first becomes a thesis. The body then spends itself proving it, and the result reads like Phase 1 draft work: thorough, safe, closed. A headline written last can point at the single sharpest thing the finished piece actually earned. That is Phase 2 work: pick what matters, create a question before answering it, find the edge.

So a headline is *derived*, not invented. You do not decide what it says and then write toward it. You finish the piece, find the line or idea that created the most tension, and point at it.

## Its one job: open a gap

A headline creates a question the body answers. The reader should wonder before they understand. This is the HOOK operation, and the skill's core principle ("create questions before you answer them"), applied to a single line.

The raw material is usually already on the page. The best headline is often the most provocative *true* statement in the finished piece, the EDGE, lifted out and set on top. You are not adding a claim. You are surfacing the one the piece already makes.

- Before (explains, closes the gap): "How our recommendation engine personalises your reading list"
- After (opens a gap): "Most reading lists grow. Most books on them never get opened."

The second one makes a reader need the next line. The first tells them they can skip it.

## How to derive it

1. Finish the piece, or read the finished piece you were handed.
2. Find the moment of highest tension: the line a reader would underline, the claim that is uncomfortable but true, the reframe that reorders what came before.
3. Point at that moment without resolving it. Name the gap, not the answer.
4. Write three to five candidates, each pulling from a different moment, so the author can pick the tension that fits.

## Channel-specific length and placement

The craft above is universal. Length and placement come from the channel the headline tops.

| Topping | Length and placement | Read |
|---------|----------------------|------|
| Web page | H1 / hero line, short, paired with a subhead that can carry the second beat | `references/channels/website.md` |
| Essay or article | Title, can run longer; let the subtitle carry the gap if the title names the territory | (the piece itself) |
| LinkedIn post | The first line *is* the headline; it has to survive the feed's truncation | `references/channels/linkedin.md` |
| Email | Subject line, roughly under 50 characters; the preview text is a second hook, not a repeat | `references/channels/email.md` |

### Email subject lines

A subject line is a headline with a hard length limit and a partner. Write it last, like any headline, and open a gap. Then write the preview (preheader) text as a *second* hook that extends the subject rather than repeating it. Keep the subject roughly under 50 characters so it survives a phone inbox. For everything below the subject (opening, body, the one call to action, sign-off), read `references/channels/email.md`.

## Cadence: headlines are the highest-risk position

Every machine-made cadence tell is most tempting, and most visible, in a headline. The negation reveal ("It's not X. It's Y.") is the single most common AI headline shape, and the aphoristic mic-drop ("Just clarity.") is close behind. One per piece is the budget, and the headline is the loudest place to spend it, so spend it only when the plain version cannot do the job. Run the headline through `references/cadence.md` like any other line. A headline that reads as a template is worse than a plain one, because it is the first thing the reader sees.

## Anti-patterns

| Headline wouldn't | Headline would |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Label the page ("About our product", "Introduction") | Open a question the page answers |
| Summarise the whole piece in one flat line | Point at the one thing worth reading for |
| Promise what the body never pays off (clickbait) | Surface a tension the body genuinely resolves |
| Reach for a borrowed "deep" cadence | Say the sharp true thing plainly |

Clickbait and EDGE are not the same. EDGE is a provocative statement that is *true* and that the body delivers on. Clickbait is a gap the body never closes. The test: would the writer stand behind this line after the reader finishes the piece?

## Quality checks

- [ ] Written after the body, derived from a real moment in the finished piece.
- [ ] Opens a gap: the reader has to want the next line.
- [ ] The body pays it off (edge, not clickbait).
- [ ] Archetype voice is recognisable in the one line (verified against the archetype's Sentence Patterns and Language Domain).
- [ ] Within the topping channel's length and placement rules.
- [ ] CEFR ceiling respected: the sharpest headline is usually the plainest.
- [ ] Passes `references/cadence.md`: not a templated negation reveal or mic-drop.
- [ ] Read aloud: a person could have said this, and would stand behind it after reading the piece.
